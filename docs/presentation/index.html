Welcome. Simujoules is a free, build-step-free web app that computes the *energy* it costs to cycle across real terrain. The motif is the app's own icon — concentric energy isolines, deliberately skewed south-east because the cost of cycling is asymmetric: climbing is expensive, descending is nearly free. That asymmetry is the whole talk in one picture. Everything runs client-side; the elevation data never leaves your machine.

Motivation A cyclist's real cost is effort, not kilometres

On hilly terrain — São Paulo is the home case — the shortest or fastest route can be the most exhausting.

  • Flat is cheap.
  • Climbing is expensive — you fight gravity.
  • Descending partly refunds energy (coasting), but only partly.

So the natural objective is mechanical energy, and the cost is:

  • Directional — A→B ≠ B→A.
  • Asymmetric — up and down are not mirror images.

Distance- and time-models miss exactly this. Energy captures the effort a rider actually feels.

Out of this falls a richer question: where does terrain itself force cyclists to converge?

The framing question. A flat 6 km route can be easier than a hilly 4 km one. Because climbing and descending are not symmetric, the cost graph is *directed* — and that single fact drives the whole design: the "to-source" reverse field, the round-trip mode, the directed bridge portals. Keep the asymmetry in mind; it recurs everywhere.

The asymmetric insight

Every metre travelled costs α·d. Climbing adds β·Δh. A descent gives back only η of what the climb cost — never more, never below zero.

Three forces. A baseline cost per metre travelled (rolling resistance, air, the act of pedalling) — that's α·d, always positive. Climbing adds β·Δh, proportional to height gained. A descent refunds, but only a fraction η of the equivalent climb, and the edge cost is clamped at zero — you can coast for free but the terrain can never *pay* you to ride. η is typically small (0.1), reflecting that most of a descent's potential energy is lost to braking, drag and not-pedalling.

The cost model one equation, shared by every algorithm and both engines

For a directed edge u → v over ground distance d, with Δh = hv − hu:

cost = α·d + β·Δh   if Δh ≥ 0  (uphill / flat)
cost = max(0,  α·d − η·β·|Δh|)   if Δh < 0  (downhill)
α cost per flat metre
default 0.008 (≈ 8 mJ/m)
β cost per metre climbed
default 1.0 (≈ 1 J/m)
η ∈ [0,1] descent recovery
default 0.1 (10% refunded)

One energy unit ≈ the work to climb 1 m ≈ 0.8–1 kJ mechanical, for an ~85 kg rider + bike. A deliberately rough cyclist model — not a calibrated physiological one.

This is the heart. Note the model is honest about its scope: a rough effort proxy, not a physiological power model. The defaults make β=1 the natural unit — one unit of energy is one metre of climb — and α=0.008 means flat distance is cheap relative to climbing, so the optimiser will happily take a longer flat detour over a short steep one. This exact code appears in *every* routine: Dijkstra, the density engine, A*, the max-cost DP, the bridge portals, and the graph engine. One model, reused — which is what makes cross-mode consistency provable.

Origin "a static-site, no-backend port of a QGIS Processing algorithm"

  • First architecture bet: a Rust→WebAssembly inner loop with a pure-JS fallback (for speed at scale).
  • The wasm bet was abandoned 8 days later (2026-05-13) — JS Web Workers became the sole engine.
  • Part of pedalhidrografi.co; deploys to a GCS bucket behind Cloud CDN.

Two roads to "native"

Dropped: wasm in-page engine — complexity for a speed-up that the pure-JS path, once tuned, mostly recovered.

Kept (later): an opt-in Rust+rayon HTTP backend — native speed when you want it, never required.

A research prototype that turned into a maintained tool — with the engineering discipline to match.

The lineage matters for an academic audience: this started life as a QGIS Processing algorithm. The web port was meant to remove the install barrier. The wasm story is instructive — the first instinct was "drop to native for speed," but they backed it out and instead invested in making the JS engine fast (typed arrays, a hand-rolled binary heap, later a radix heap). Native came back, but as an *optional* server, never a dependency. That "optional, with automatic fallback" pattern recurs across every heavy feature.

Methodology I the energy field

Run Dijkstra on an 8-connected grid (4 cardinal + 4 diagonal) from an anchor point. For every passable cell you get the minimum energy to reach it.

Three modes — all needed because the graph is directed:

  • from forward field — cost to ride out to each cell
  • to reverse search — cost to ride back to the anchor
  • round forward + reverse, summed per cell

Numerical care

Energies stored as Float32 (memory), but heap priorities and comparisons are Float64.

A per-cell settled byte — not g > E[idx] — filters stale heap entries. Drop either guard and the f32/f64 mismatch corrupts the passes counts and caps the field at ~200 m.

Standard Dijkstra, but the "to" mode is the non-obvious one: because cost is directional, the energy to get *back* is a genuinely different field, so it gets its own reverse search (edges flipped). Round-trip sums them. The numerical-care box is a real war story: storing energy in f32 halves memory on a 135-million-cell grid, but then your heap priorities (f64) disagree with stored values at the bit level, so you must gate on an explicit settled flag, not on comparing tentative-vs-stored. They learned that the hard way.

Methodology II passes & "natural corridors"

Passes count at a cell = its subtree size in the shortest-path tree: how many optimal paths cross it.

Computed by walking the settle order in reverse, adding each cell's count to its parent.

It reveals the terrain's natural highways — saddles, valley floors, the places topology forces movement to funnel through — without ever prescribing a road network.

This is the conceptual payoff. A single energy field tells you the cost to one anchor. But passes — betweenness-like subtree sizes — tell you where *many* paths converge. In hilly terrain that's the saddles and valley floors. It's an emergent property of topography, not an input. That's why I call them "natural corridors." Round-trip mode only counts destinations you could actually complete within budget.

Methodology III multi-reference density + quasi-random sampling

Density = passes counts accumulated over K reference points and normalised. A (quasi-)Monte-Carlo estimate of how strongly terrain channels movement, region-wide.

References can be clicked, pseudo-random, or quasi-random (QMC) — Sobol or Halton sequences.

Why QMC?

Sobol/Halton cover the area evenly, without the clumps and gaps of pseudo-random — so the density field converges with fewer references. Successive batches continue the same sequence.

Density is the perf-critical path — and the reason the whole compute architecture (next) exists.

Quasi-Monte-Carlo is a small but genuine bit of rigour. Pseudo-random points clump; Sobol and Halton are low-discrepancy, so they tile the area evenly and the density estimate converges faster — fewer Dijkstra runs for the same picture. The implementation builds Sobol direction numbers from a primitive polynomial; Halton uses bases 2 and 3. Each reference is an independent Dijkstra, which is exactly why it parallelises.

Methodology IV alternatives, and the opposite question

Top-N routes

Run A* repeatedly with a repulsion penalty on already-used cells (per-cell / linear / quadratic) so alternatives genuinely diverge.

The penalty multiplies only the α·dist term — climb is never penalised (it's unavoidable). Admissible heuristic: α·straight + β·max(0, Δhgoal).

Maximize mode

Invert the objective — find the most expensive routes — by relaxing on maxEdgeCost − cost.

Optionally length-constrained: a layered DP finds the max-cost path of exactly L edges (Bellman-Ford-style, capped at 256 MB of parent pointers).

Plus energy budgets (eMax) that prune the search — per-leg or round-trip-total.

Two extras. Top-N uses iterative penalisation — re-run A*, charge more for cells you've already used, get a spatially distinct alternative. Crucially the penalty hits only the distance term, never the climb, because you can't wish a hill away. Maximize is the dual problem — the hardest ride the terrain affords — useful for stress-testing or "what's the worst case." The length-constrained version is a layered DP, and it's memory-bounded because exactly-L-edge tables get huge fast.

Systems — two compute tiers

One worker

Point-to-point runs (from/to/round, top-N, maximize): a single Web Worker. Simple, responsive.

A memory-budgeted pool

Density fans K independent Dijkstras across a worker pool. Each worker returns raw accumulators; the main thread merges & normalises.

poolSize = min( K, cores−1, memCap )

memCap budgets ~38 B/cell (≈55 round) against navigator.deviceMemory — which the spec caps at 8 GB.

The 135 M-cell ceiling. On sampa_geral (14913×9055 ≈ 135 M cells), one density worker needs ≈ 5 GB; two won't fit 16 GB — so the pool honestly collapses to one worker. A #max-workers override exists for big-RAM machines the spec can't see.
The split is principled: a single point-to-point query doesn't parallelise (it's one Dijkstra), but density is K independent Dijkstras — embarrassingly parallel. The pool sizing is memory-aware, not just core-aware, because each worker needs its own copy of grid-sized scratch. The "honest ceiling" point is one I like: on the biggest DEM the tool tells you the truth — it's one worker, because two would OOM. It doesn't pretend to parallelism it can't deliver. deviceMemory being capped at 8 GB by the spec is why the manual override exists.

Systems — the optional native backend

  • Rust + rayon HTTP server — off by default, enabled per-session, with automatic fallback to browser workers on any failure.
  • References split into per-thread slices, each reusing one set of scratch buffers (no per-ref allocation).
  • Priority queue: a radix heap on raw f64 bits — exact minima, no quantisation.
  • Concurrent slices capped to a memory budget — high ref counts run more serially rather than OOM-crash.

At full saturation both are memory-bandwidth-bound, so native and JS converge on huge frontiers.

The backend is an opt-in accelerator, never required — tick a box, it streams the grid over a binary protocol, computes on all cores, streams the field back; the moment it's unreachable the app silently uses browser workers. The radix heap on raw IEEE-754 bits is a nice trick: f64 keys are order-preserving as unsigned ints, so you get exact-minimum extraction in O(1) push / amortised O(64) pop with no float quantisation. The honest caveat: on the biggest grids both engines are limited by memory bandwidth, not CPU, so the native advantage shrinks toward ~1.3×.

One model, two engines — bit-parity by construction

backend/src/main.rs is a line-for-line port of energy-worker.js's dijkstra(). The optimisation must never change the science — only the speed.

|Δenergy| < 1e-3

the Float32 storage floor

|Δdensity| < 1e-15

machine epsilon

0 mismatches

finite ↔ infinite

Passes counts may differ only where two paths have exactly equal f64 cost — the radix heap and the binary heap break ties in different orders, and either optimal tree is valid.

node backend/test-backend.mjs enforces this across from/to/round × budgets × bridge portals.

This is the engineering thesis of the project: you can have a fast native path *and* trust it, because it's provably the same computation. Energies match to the f32 storage floor (1e-3); density matches to machine epsilon. The only permitted divergence is passes counts on *exact* f64 ties — where two distinct shortest-path trees are both genuinely optimal and the two heaps just pick different parents. The test asserts this on every mode. The discipline is written into CLAUDE.md: change one engine, you must change the other, and the test gates the commit.

The 2.5-D problem the project's deepest contribution

A bare-earth DEM stores one elevation per cell. Two things it cannot represent:

water The grid happily routes a bike across a lake. There's nothing marking "you can't go here."
bridges A viaduct deck is ~flat, but the DEM shows the valley under it. Routing over the bridge dives into the gap and pays a phantom climb.

The crux: a bridge over a road is two routable surfaces at the same (row, col).

Here's where it gets genuinely hard and genuinely novel. A DEM is 2.5-D — a heightfield, one z per (x,y). That's fine until you have water (an absence of a "no-go" concept) or a bridge (a place where two surfaces coexist vertically — the deck and the ground beneath). The viaduct case is the killer: the deck should read flat, but a bare-earth model shows the saddle below it, so the optimiser thinks crossing the bridge means descending into a gully and climbing out. Both problems were solved in v18 and v19.

Three approaches, one winner

ApproachIdeaVerdict
Raster cell-overrideOverwrite the deck cells' height to the deck✗ raises the ground at the crossing — breaks the road underneath
Graph modeRoute on the vector network, deck & road as separate edges◐ works, JS-only — kept for "follow the vectors", deferred for raster
Hybrid portal edgesKeep the dense raster and inject one shortcut per bridge✓ both levels stay correct simultaneously

The hybrid is the only raster approach where the route over the bridge and the route under it are both correct — on the same grid, at once.

They investigated three options. Cell-override is the naive fix — just raise the deck cells — and it's wrong precisely at the interesting cell: raising the deck raises the ground for the road passing underneath, breaking that route. Graph mode works but is a different engine (the vector path). The winner is the hybrid: keep the full ground raster so the under-route is untouched, and add a *portal*.

The portal model

A directed shortcut A↔B at the flat-deck cost, relaxed alongside the 8 grid edges. The cells under the deck are never touched — so the ground line C–D still routes through the valley, unchanged.

The portal is one directed edge between the two abutment cells, priced at the flat-deck cost using the *same* asymmetric model. It's relaxed right after the eight grid neighbours, with the same settled/budget/maximize guards. The genius is what it *doesn't* do: it never modifies the cells beneath the deck. So the over-route (A→B) gets its cheap flat crossing, and the under-route (C→D) still sees the real ground. Two levels, one raster. And because the portal cost is derived from the same shared inputs in JS and Rust, it stays bit-parity — the test has +portals cases.

Passability — pulling the world from OpenStreetMap

Bridges & tunnels (v19/v23)

Overpass query for highway bridges (way[bridge][highway]) / tunnels over the DEM extent. OSM splits a bridge way exactly at its abutments — free endpoints. v23 also reads node ele tags for true deck heights.

Impassable water (v18/v22)

Upload a binary mask, or pull from OSM:

  • water areas — even-odd polygon fill, islands → holes
  • open sea — from coastlines (land-left/water-right) via an orientation sweep, so gaps never leak
  • rivers — supercover lines an 8-connected route can't slip across

All composed app-side, so the parity-locked engine stays untouched — the no-op invariant: no mask, no bridges ⇒ byte-identical to before.

You don't need to bring your own GIS data — the tool pulls bridges and water straight from OpenStreetMap over the DEM's footprint. Two nice details: OSM conveniently splits bridge ways at the abutments, so the geometry hands you the ground endpoints for free; and the sea fill can't just flood-fill (a single coastline gap would leak the ocean across all land), so it uses the coastline's directed orientation — water is always to the right — swept horizontally and vertically. The whole passability layer is composed before the engine sees it, preserving bit-parity.

This is the money slide for the 2.5-D claim. A real viaduct on Av. Dr. Arnaldo: the deck sits at 814 m, but the bare-earth DEM dips to 788.7 m in the gap below — a 25-metre phantom valley. Add the portal and the energy to reach the far abutment drops from 25.18 to 1.33 units — about 18.9× — which is exactly α times the 173-metre flat deck. And the orthogonal ground line crossing underneath? Bit-for-bit unchanged. That Δ=0 is the proof that the two levels genuinely coexist; we fixed the over-route without touching the under-route. About 44% of the wider field shifts too — expected, because the bridge is a real shortcut that opens cheap downstream access.

A performance model predicting compute time before you run

Density runs can take seconds to many minutes. The app shows a pre-flight estimate — backed by a validated analytic model, not a fudge factor.

T ≈ calloc·N  +  R · τref(e) / S(Peff)
τref(e) = (N/r) · min(1, (e/efull)²) · ν
per-reference cost — quadratic in budget, then saturates
S(P) = P / (1 + b(P−1))
parallel speed-up with bandwidth contention b

Time is linear in references R, quadratic-then-flat in the energy budget, and divided by threads.

The estimator is itself a small research result. Density cost decomposes into a one-time scratch allocation (c_alloc·N) plus per-reference Dijkstra work, divided by parallelism. The interesting term is τ_ref: exploration grows quadratically with the energy budget — because reachable *area* scales as reach-squared and reach scales with budget over α — until it saturates the whole grid. The measured exponent is 2.0–2.1, right on the geometric prediction. So doubling the budget quadruples the work, until you've filled the DEM and it flatlines.

The coupling that fooled the estimate

Peff = min( P,   R,   ⌊(M − 21N) / 38N⌋ )

Usable threads are capped not by cores but by memory — each worker/slice needs grid-sized scratch. On 135 M cells: 1 worker ≈ 8 GB, 2 ≈ 13 GB → two won't fit 16 GB.

Anatomy of a 3.63× bug. The old backend estimate assumed 8-way parallelism — but the server only fits 2 memory-bounded slices on a huge DEM. Combined with a too-low native constant, it under-estimated by 3.63×. The fix: replicate the slice cap in the estimate.
Here's the bug that motivated the whole investigation. Users reported the estimate reading ~3× low on the big DEM with the native backend. The root cause: the estimate assumed parallelism scaled with core count, but each rayon slice needs ~5 GB of scratch, so only 2 fit in the memory budget regardless of having 10 cores. The model divided work by 8 when reality divided by 2 — a 4× error, partly offset, netting 3.63×. The lesson is that memory, not cores, governs parallelism at scale — the P↔M coupling — and the estimate has to model it.

In production: a per-DEM calibration probe at load (3 refs, capped by settled cells not budget → always under ~1.5 s and always unsaturated), plus a per-engine online EMA correction.

First-run accuracy ±20% → converges to ~1% within a run or two. Residual ±30% is dominated by which references you place, not the machine.

And it validates: 426 predicted vs 427 measured is almost suspiciously good, but it holds across the matrix. The production system can't hardcode constants like r or e_full because they're terrain-dependent, so it runs a tiny calibration probe when you load a DEM — three spread references, stopped after a fixed *cell count* rather than a budget, which guarantees it finishes fast and, crucially, anchors at an unsaturated point. Then a per-engine exponential-moving-average correction learns the residual from each completed run. First estimate within 20%, then ~1%. The honest caveat: the dominant remaining error is *which* references you pick — a flat-valley reference explores far more than a ridge-boxed one.

Validation & verification

Four test suites, run before every engine commit (there is no CI):

  • test-worker-pool.mjs — invariants + pooled ≡ single-run density (maxΔ = 0) + portal regression
  • test-backend.mjs — JS↔Rust bit-parity, all modes × portals
  • test-water-raster.mjs — OSM water rasterisation vs pure mirrors
  • test-graph-engine.mjs — planarisation + a 400-trial crossing fuzz

Two keystones

The no-op invariant. A run with no mask and no bridges is byte-identical to the pre-feature engine — so passability could ship without re-validating the parity-locked core.

Adversarial review as process. The v18 engine change was reviewed by 11 agents → 6 real issues, 0 false positives.

Correctness is treated as bit-level parity, not approximate agreement — verified structurally, not just sampled.

Validation philosophy: correctness here means *bit parity*, not "close enough." The pooled-vs-single test proves that fanning density across a worker pool introduces literally zero error (maxΔ = 0). The no-op invariant is the architectural keystone — every new feature is built so that with it switched off, the output is byte-identical to before, which means you never have to re-validate the core. And the development process itself is part of the evidence: much of this was built with an AI coding agent, but under a strict regime of parity tests and adversarial multi-agent review — 11 reviewers on the v18 change found 6 real bugs and raised zero false alarms. That's the kind of rigour that makes me trust it.

Privacy by architecture

Pure client-side compute — your DEM never leaves the browser. No account, no upload, works offline after first load.

Build-step-free PWA: index.html + app.js + Web Workers · five CDN libraries, SRI-pinned · service-worker offline · PT/EN.

The open-science story. Every run exports a self-contained zip: georeferenced GeoTIFFs that drop straight into QGIS, GeoJSON for the vector layers, and a JSON-LD metadata file capturing every single parameter — so anyone with the same DEM can reproduce the run exactly. The metadata isn't ad-hoc: there's a published OWL ontology with QUDT units and a CC-BY licence. And the whole thing is privacy-preserving by construction — no server-side compute, your elevation data never leaves your machine, and it's a no-build PWA that works offline. For a research tool, that combination — reproducible, interoperable, private, zero-install — is the contribution as much as the algorithms are.

Limitations stated honestly

  • Geographic-CRS bias. Map/click/overlays assume EPSG:4326; degrees→metres is a flat-earth approximation (good to ~0.3% under 50 km).
  • Rough cost model. α/β/η is an effort proxy, not a calibrated power/physiology model.
  • Portals are scoped. A* top-N, the max-cost DP, and maximize mode don't use portals (admissibility / degeneracy).
  • Memory-bound at scale. 135 M cells = one worker on 16 GB. Bigger needs more RAM or tiling.
  • Estimate caveats. ±20% first run; reference placement dominates the residual.
  • OSM-dependence. Bridge/water quality is OSM quality; the pull needs a geographic DEM.
I want to be upfront about scope. The CRS handling is the biggest one — it's really a WGS84 tool, and horizontal distances use a flat-earth cosine approximation that's fine at city scale but not for large or high-latitude extents. The cost model is a proxy, deliberately. Portals are powerful but they're only wired into the Dijkstra-based modes, not A* or the DP, because a shortcut breaks an admissible straight-line heuristic. And it's memory-bound at the top end. None of these are hidden — they're documented in the README and CLAUDE.md.

Future work

Routing

  • Graph-mode portals (carry bridge/layer tags fully into "follow the vectors")
  • Portals in A*/DP via an admissible shortcut-aware heuristic
  • Layer-aware portals (filter by span length / layer)

Modelling & scale

  • Calibrate α/β/η against measured rider power / Strava data
  • Model remote-backend HTTP transfer explicitly in the estimate
  • Tiling / hierarchical Dijkstra beyond the single-worker ceiling

The architecture — composed app-side, bit-parity engines, no-op invariants — is built to absorb these without destabilising the core.

Where it goes next. On the routing side: extend the portal model fully into graph mode, find an admissible heuristic so A* and the DP can use portals, and make portals selective by span/layer. On the modelling side, the most scientifically interesting one is calibrating α, β, η against real rider data — Strava, power meters — to turn the effort proxy into something quantitative. And to break the memory ceiling you'd want tiling or a hierarchical Dijkstra. The point of the careful architecture is that all of these can be added behind the same no-op-invariant discipline.

Takeaways

  1. Energy, asymmetric and directional, is the right cost for cycling on terrain — and it makes "natural corridors" computable.
  2. Hybrid portal edges solve genuine 2.5-D multi-level routing on a bare-earth raster — proven Δ=0 under a real viaduct.
  3. A validated analytic performance model turns "it'll be a while" into a calibrated, self-correcting ±20% estimate.
  4. Bit-parity, no-op invariants, reproducible JSON-LD bundles — fast, optional acceleration that never changes the science.

A privacy-preserving, zero-install, reproducible geospatial energy-field tool — that scales to 135 M cells in a browser tab.

Four things to remember. One: energy is the right objective for cycling, and asymmetry is the load-bearing idea. Two: the portal trick is a clean, verifiable answer to 2.5-D routing on raster DEMs. Three: the performance model is a real, validated result, not a guess. Four: the whole thing is built so that speed never costs correctness, and every run is reproducible. Thank you — questions welcome.
